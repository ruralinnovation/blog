Overview

The Business Dynamics Statistics (BDS) provides annual measures of business openings and closings, job creation and destruction, and employment changes by firm age and size. Built from the Census Bureau’s Longitudinal Business Database (LBD), BDS tracks the universe of employer businesses with paid employees.

BDS is essential for understanding entrepreneurship, business dynamism, and the role of young firms in job creation. It uniquely measures firm age (not just establishment age), enabling analysis of startup ecosystems and firm lifecycle dynamics.

NoteWhy BDS is Essential
  • Universe data - Administrative records, not survey (no sampling error)
  • Firm age tracking - Unique ability to distinguish true startups from branch openings
  • Job flow measures - Gross creation/destruction reveal churn, not just net change
  • Historical depth - National data back to 1977 (county data from 2007)

Coverage

Geographic Coverage

  • County
  • Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)
  • State
  • National

Temporal Coverage

  • 1977-present (national; county from 2007)
  • Annual updates
  • Released ~2 years after reference year
  • Universe: All employer businesses

Key Variables

Business Counts

  • Establishments - Total number of business establishments
  • Firms - Number of separate business entities
  • Establishment Entry Rate - % of establishments that are new entrants (<1 year)
  • Establishment Exit Rate - % of establishments that closed in last 12 months

Employment

  • Total Employment - Jobs at all establishments
  • Employment by Firm Age - Jobs at firms 0-5, 6-10, 11+ years old
  • Employment by Firm Size - Jobs at firms with <20, 20-499, 500+ employees

Job Flows

  • Job Creation - Gross jobs created (new businesses + expanding businesses)
  • Job Destruction - Gross jobs lost (closing businesses + contracting businesses)
  • Net Job Creation - Job creation minus job destruction
  • Job Creation Rate - Job creation as % of average employment

Firm Age Dynamics (CORI Focus)

  • Share of Job Creation from Young Firms (0-5 years) - % of all new jobs from startups
  • Share from Mid-Age Firms (6-10 years) - % from scaling businesses
  • Share from Mature Firms (11+ years) - % from established businesses

Data Structure

Standard Format:

  • One row per geography per firm characteristic per year
  • GEOID: 5-digit county FIPS code
  • Firm age/size bins as separate rows or columns

Typical Variables:

Methodology

Longitudinal Business Database (LBD)

BDS is derived from the LBD, which links:

  • IRS business tax returns - Employer Identification Numbers (EINs)
  • Social Security Administration payroll records - Quarterly employment
  • Economic Census - Industry classifications

Firm vs. Establishment

Establishment

A single physical location (e.g., one retail store)

Firm

A business entity that may own multiple establishments (e.g., retail chain)

BDS measures firm age, not establishment age. This captures:

  • True startups (new firms, not just new branches)
  • Firm lifecycle dynamics
  • Entrepreneurial activity vs. corporate expansion

Entry and Exit Rates

Entry Rate = (New establishments in year t) / (Establishments in year t) × 100

Exit Rate = (Establishments that closed in last 12 months) / (Establishments in year t-1) × 100

Job Creation and Destruction

Job Creation = Σ(employment growth at expanding/new establishments)
Job Destruction = Σ(employment decline at contracting/closing establishments)
Net Job Creation = Job Creation - Job Destruction

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Universe data (no sampling error)
  • Firm age tracking (unique)
  • Job flow measures
  • Historical depth (1977+)
  • Consistent methodology

Limitations

  • Lagged release (~2 years)
  • County data starts 2007
  • Excludes non-employers
  • Disclosure suppression
  • Limited industry detail at county level

Important Notes

Young Firms and Job Creation

TipResearch Findings

Research (Haltiwanger, Jarmin, Miranda) shows:

  • Young firms (0-5 years) create disproportionate share of new jobs
  • After controlling for firm age, firm size has little effect on job creation rates
  • Startups have high failure rates but survivors grow rapidly

BDS enables this analysis by separating firm age from firm size.

Rural Entrepreneurship Patterns

CORI analysis finds:

  • Rural areas often have lower entry rates but similar exit rates
  • Young firm job creation share varies widely across rural counties
  • Counties with strong young firm dynamics tend to have better economic momentum

Relationship to BFS

BDS measures employer businesses (firms with paid employees), while Census BFS measures business applications (intent to start business).

Pipeline: BFS applications → (18 months) → BDS employer firms
