Overview

The United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) grants patents for inventions. PatentsView is a data platform that makes USPTO patent data accessible for research and analysis, including geographic attribution to inventor and assignee locations.

Patent counts serve as an innovation indicator, revealing which regions are producing new technologies, products, and processes. Patent data is especially valuable for tracking:

  • Regional innovation capacity
  • Technology clusters
  • University and corporate research output
  • Rural innovation ecosystems
NoteWhy Patent Data Matters
  • Objective innovation measure - Patents require rigorous examination
  • Long time series - Data back to 1976
  • Technology detail - Understand what is being invented
  • Geographic precision - County-level attribution
  • Linked data - Citations, inventors, assignees, classifications

Key Variables

Patent Counts

  • Total Patents - Count of patents granted
  • Patents by Technology Field - CPC/IPC classification
  • Patents by Assignee Type - Corporate, university, government, individual

Patent Details

  • Patent Number - Unique identifier
  • Grant Date - Date patent was granted
  • Filing Date - Date application was filed (lag: 2-3 years)
  • Inventor Location - Geographic location of inventors
  • Assignee Location - Geographic location of patent owner (company, university)

Citation Metrics

  • Citations Received - How many later patents cite this patent (measure of influence)
  • Citations Made - Prior art references

Methodology

Geographic Attribution

Patents can be attributed to locations in multiple ways:

Inventor Location

Where inventors reside (can be multiple inventors per patent)

Assignee Location

Where patent owner is located (company HQ, university)

CORI uses assignee location because:

  • Reflects where innovation benefits accrue (jobs, revenue)
  • More stable over time (inventors may move)
  • Aligns with economic development goals

Fractional Counting

When multiple inventors/assignees exist:

  • Each location receives fractional credit
  • Example: Patent with 2 inventors in different counties → 0.5 patents counted for each county

Technology Classification

Patents classified using:

  • CPC (Cooperative Patent Classification) - 250,000+ classifications
  • IPC (International Patent Classification) - Global standard
  • NBER Technology Categories - 37 high-level categories (computers, pharma, etc.)

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Objective innovation measure
  • Long time series (1976+)
  • Technology detail
  • Geographic precision
  • Linked data (citations, etc.)

Limitations

  • Not all innovation patented
  • Industry variation
  • Quality variation
  • Lag from invention (2-3 years)
  • Low counts in rural areas
  • Assignee location bias

Important Notes

Rural Patent Patterns

WarningLow Rural Patent Counts

Rural counties typically have very low patent counts:

  • 50% of rural counties: 0-1 patents per year
  • Patent activity concentrated in:
    • University counties (land-grant institutions)
    • Manufacturing hubs (ag equipment, natural resources)
    • Military installations (defense technology)

Interpretation: Patent counts are not suitable as a standalone rural innovation measure.

Combine with:

  • Business Dynamics Statistics - Entrepreneurship
  • Business Formation Statistics - Startup activity
  • Industry employment in knowledge-intensive sectors

Patents vs. Innovation

Patents measure formal, technical innovation. Many forms of innovation are not patented:

  • Business model innovation
  • Process improvements
  • Service innovation
  • Open-source software

Rural innovation may be underrepresented in patent data.

Citation Analysis

Citation counts reveal patent influence:

  • Highly Cited Patents
  • Zero Citations
  • Rural Context

Foundational innovations with significant impact

Limited impact or very recent patents

Rural patents tend to have fewer citations on average, potentially indicating:

  • Applied vs. fundamental research
  • Narrower market applications
  • Lower visibility in patenting community

Usage Example

library(patentsview)

# Search for patents in a specific technology area
tech_patents <- search_pv(
  query = qry_funs$and(
    qry_funs$gte(patent_date = "2020-01-01"),
    qry_funs$text_phrase(cpc_subgroup_id = "G06N")  # AI/ML patents
  ),
  fields = c("patent_number", "patent_date", "assignee_organization"),
  endpoint = "patents"
)

# Get inventor locations
inventor_locs <- search_pv(
  query = qry_funs$eq(inventor_state = "VT"),
  fields = c("patent_number", "inventor_city", "inventor_county"),
  endpoint = "patents"
)
