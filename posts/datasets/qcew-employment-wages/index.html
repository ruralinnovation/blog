Overview

The Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW) is a comprehensive dataset covering 95% of U.S. jobs. Unlike survey-based employment data, QCEW is a census based on mandatory quarterly unemployment insurance (UI) tax filings from employers.

QCEW provides employment counts and wage data at detailed industry levels (6-digit NAICS codes) for every county in the U.S., making it the gold standard for understanding local labor markets and economic structure.

NoteWhy QCEW is Essential
  • Census data - Not a survey, covers 95% of U.S. jobs
  • Industry detail - 6-digit NAICS codes (1,000+ industries)
  • Historical depth - Consistent data back to 1990

Coverage

Geographic Coverage - County level - Metropolitan/Micropolitan Areas - State level - National totals

Temporal Coverage - 1990-present (quarterly and annual) - Quarterly updates (~6-month lag) - Annual averages (recommended for trends)

Universe: 95% of U.S. jobs (excludes self-employed, farm workers, household workers)

Wage Metrics

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index measuring industry concentration (0-10,000 scale):

# HHI calculation
HHI = sum((employment_share_i)^2) * 10000
  • HHI < 1,500: Unconcentrated economy
  • HHI 1,500-2,500: Moderate concentration
  • HHI > 2,500: High concentration
  • Tradable Goods - Manufacturing, mining, agriculture
  • Tradable Services - Information, finance, professional services
  • Local Services - Retail, healthcare, hospitality

Methodology

Data Collection

QCEW is built from three sources:

graph LR
    A[UI-Covered Employers<br/>Quarterly UI-3 Forms] --> D[QCEW Database]
    B[Federal Agencies<br/>USPS, Military] --> D
    C[Railroad Retirement Board<br/>Railroad Workers] --> D
    D --> E[95% of Civilian<br/>Employment]

WarningExcluded from QCEW
  • Self-employed individuals (no employees)
  • Farm workers (some states)
  • Household workers (domestic employees)
  • Unincorporated businesses

Wage Deflation (CORI Method)

Nominal wages converted to real 2023 dollars using BLS CPI-U-RS:

Show deflation function 
deflate_wages <- function(nominal_wage, year, base_year = 2023) {
  # CPI-U-RS values (example)
  cpi_values <- c(
    "2020" = 306.8,
    "2021" = 319.4,
    "2022" = 348.0,
    "2023" = 360.0
  )

  real_wage <- nominal_wage * (cpi_values[as.character(base_year)] /
                                cpi_values[as.character(year)])
  return(real_wage)
}

Why CPI-U-RS? More consistent for historical comparisons than standard CPI-U.

Strengths & Limitations

✅ Strengths

  • Census, not survey (no sampling error)
  • 95% job coverage
  • 6-digit NAICS industry detail
  • Historical depth (1990+)
  • Quarterly frequency
  • Official data source

⚠️ Limitations

  • 6-month lag in release
  • Excludes self-employment
  • Disclosure suppression (<3 establishments)
  • Location of work, not residence
  • Nominal wages (need deflation)
  • Quarterly volatility

Important Notes

Disclosure Rules

ImportantPrivacy Protection

To protect business confidentiality: - Industries with fewer than 3 establishments suppressed - Rural counties often have more suppression than urban - Solution: Use 2-digit NAICS for consistent county coverage

BEA Table Replacement

As of 2023, QCEW is the recommended source for county employment after 2022:

BEA Table Status Replacement
CAEMP25N (Employment by industry) ❌ Discontinued after 2022 ✅ QCEW
CAINC6N (Compensation by industry) ❌ Discontinued after 2022 ✅ QCEW

QCEW provides comparable industry breakdowns with more frequent updates.
