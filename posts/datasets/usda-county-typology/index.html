Overview

The USDA Economic Research Service (ERS) County Typology Codes classify counties into economic and demographic categories based on various characteristics. These classifications help researchers and policymakers:

  • Understand county economic specialization
  • Identify persistent challenges (poverty, education, employment)
  • Stratify analysis by county type
  • Target policies to specific county contexts

The typology provides a static snapshot of county characteristics, typically updated every 10 years following the decennial census.

NoteWhy Typology Codes Matter
  • Mutually exclusive categories - Every county has one economic type
  • Research-validated - Widely used in rural studies
  • Persistent poverty flag - Identifies long-term challenges
  • Multiple dimensions - Economic type + social/demographic flags
  • Free and accessible - Easy download, no API needed

Coverage

Geographic Coverage

  • All 3,100+ U.S. counties

Temporal Coverage

  • 2025 edition (based on 2015-2023 data)
  • Previous editions: 2015, 2004, 1989
  • Updated roughly decennial

Key Classifications

Economic Typology (6 Mutually Exclusive Types)

Counties classified by dominant economic activity:

  • Farming-Dependent
  • Mining-Dependent
  • Manufacturing-Dependent
  • Government-Dependent
  • Recreation
  • Nonspecialized

Farming contributed ≥25% of county earnings or ≥16% of employment (averaged 2017-2019)

Mining contributed ≥13% of earnings or ≥8% of employment

Manufacturing contributed ≥25% of earnings or ≥18% of employment

Federal/State Government contributed ≥14% of earnings

Recreation employment ≥11% of total employment OR per capita personal income from recreation ≥11% of county income

Does not meet criteria for any specialized category (residual category)

Note: ~60-70% of counties are nonspecialized

Persistent Poverty (Binary Flag)

Counties where ≥20% of population was poor in each of the last 4 decennial censuses:

  • 1980, 1990, 2000, 2010 (for 2015 edition)
  • 1990, 2000, 2010, 2020 (for 2025 edition)

Identifies counties with long-term, structural poverty, not temporary economic shocks.

Persistent Child Poverty (Binary Flag)

Counties where ≥20% of children (age 0-17) were poor in each of the last 4 censuses.

Child poverty often more severe and has longer-lasting impacts than adult poverty.

Low Education (Binary Flag)

Counties where:

  • ≥20% of adults (25-64) lack high school diploma AND
  • <20% have bachelor’s degree or higher

Measured as of 2015-2019 ACS (2025 edition).

Low Employment (Binary Flag)

Counties with employment/population ratio <55% for working-age adults (21-64), averaged 2017-2019.

Indicates weak labor market attachment beyond unemployment (includes discouraged workers, disability).

Population Loss (Binary Flag)

Counties that lost population between 2010-2020 censuses.

Identifies long-term decline, not just short-term fluctuation.

Binary flags: 1 = meets criteria, 0 = does not meet criteria

Methodology

Economic Typology Classification

Counties assigned to types using hierarchical rules:

  1. Check farming criteria → if met, assign “Farming”
  2. If not farming, check mining → if met, assign “Mining”
  3. Continue through manufacturing, government, recreation
  4. If none met, assign “Nonspecialized”

Data Sources:

  • BEA Regional Economic Accounts (earnings by industry)
  • BLS QCEW (employment by industry)
  • Census County Business Patterns (employment)

Persistent Poverty Methodology

Using decennial census and ACS data:

  • Poverty rate calculated for all persons in county
  • Must meet 20% threshold in all 4 censuses to qualify
  • Captures structural poverty, not recession-related spikes
ImportantGeographic Distribution

~15% of U.S. counties meet persistent poverty criteria. Concentrated in:

  • Black Belt (Southeast)
  • Appalachia
  • Native American reservations
  • Texas border counties

Thresholds and Sensitivities

Thresholds (e.g., 25% for farming, 20% for persistent poverty) are somewhat arbitrary:

  • Counties just below thresholds excluded
  • Use as rough classification, not precise boundaries

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Mutually exclusive categories
  • Research-validated
  • Persistent poverty flag
  • Multiple dimensions
  • Free and accessible

Limitations

  • Static classification
  • Arbitrary thresholds
  • Mutually exclusive types (no mixed)
  • No temporal variation
  • Lagged data (2025 uses 2019 ACS)

Important Notes

Economic Typology vs. Rural Definition

WarningDifferent Concepts

ERS County Typology ≠ rural definition:

  • Rural definition (CBSA, RUCA) = urban vs. rural
  • Economic typology = type of economy

Many metro counties are farming/manufacturing-dependent. Use ruraldefinitions package for rural classification.

Nonspecialized Counties (Majority)

~60-70% of counties are “Nonspecialized”:

  • Diversified economies (no single sector dominant)
  • OR economies too small/weak to meet any threshold

Nonspecialized ≠ unimportant. Many thriving counties have diversified economies.

Persistent Poverty Concentration

Persistent poverty counties are geographically concentrated. These regions face structural challenges beyond economic cycles:

  • Historical discrimination and disinvestment
  • Limited infrastructure
  • Lower educational attainment
  • Geographic isolation

Recreation Counties

Recreation typology captures:

  • Tourism employment (hotels, restaurants, entertainment)
  • Outdoor recreation (skiing, beaches, national parks)
  • Second-home income

Rural recreation counties often have:

  • Higher property values
  • Seasonal employment
  • Income inequality (wealthy visitors vs. low-wage service workers)

Using Typology for Analysis

TipBest Practices
  1. Stratify models - Run separate regressions for farming vs. manufacturing counties
  2. Control variables - Include typology dummies in regressions
  3. Descriptive analysis - Compare outcomes across types (e.g., “Do farming counties have lower broadband adoption?”)
  4. Geographic mapping - Visualize spatial patterns of economic specialization
