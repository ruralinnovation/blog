Overview

The American Community Survey (ACS) is the largest household survey in the United States, collecting detailed demographic, social, economic, and housing information from ~3.5 million households annually. The 5-year estimates combine data from 5 consecutive years, providing reliable statistics for small geographies like counties and census tracts.

ACS replaced the decennial census “long form” in 2010, providing annual updates on topics previously available only every 10 years.

NoteWhy ACS is Essential
  • Comprehensive coverage - 40,000+ variables across all topics
  • Annual updates - More frequent than decennial census
  • Small-area estimates - County and tract-level data
  • National consistency - Comparable across all geographies

Data Structure

Standard Format:

  • One row per geography per variable
  • GEOID: 5-digit (county), 11-digit (tract), 12-digit (block group)
  • Estimates with margins of error (MOE)

Typical Variables:

geoid, year, variable, estimate, moe,
variable_label, concept

Methodology

Rolling 5-Year Averages

The 5-year ACS (e.g., 2019-2023) represents data collected over 60 months:

  • Provides larger sample size for reliable small-area estimates
  • Data represents average conditions over the period, not a specific year
  • Trade-off: Less timely than 1-year estimates but more precise

Survey Design

  • Continuous monthly sampling throughout the year
  • ~3.5 million addresses sampled annually
  • Internet, mail, phone, and in-person interviews
  • Household-based (not institutional population)

Margins of Error (MOE)

All ACS estimates include MOE at 90% confidence level:

ImportantUnderstanding Margins of Error
  • Small geographies have larger MOE
  • Use MOE to determine statistical significance
  • Aggregate across geographies to reduce MOE

Example: If estimate = 1,000 and MOE = 200, we are 90% confident the true value is between 800 and 1,200.

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  • Comprehensive coverage (40,000+ variables)
  • Small-area estimates (county and tract level)
  • Annual updates
  • Detailed demographics
  • Occupation detail (500+ codes)
  • National consistency

Limitations

  • Survey-based (sampling error/MOE)
  • 5-year lag (2019-2023 released Dec 2024)
  • Rolling averages (not single-year snapshot)
  • Small numbers unreliable (high MOE)
  • Top-coding (high incomes/values capped)
  • Disclosure avoidance (noise added)

Important Notes

Variable Consistency Across Years

TipDocumented Consistency for 2013, 2018, 2023

CORI has verified variable consistency:

  • Underlying variable codes (B-series) remain stable across years
  • Label formatting changed: “!!” separators (2013) became “::” (2018+)
  • Terminology changes: “at home” → “from home” (work location)
  • Data is comparable despite cosmetic label changes

See documentation:

  • /data/acs_variable_check.csv - Verification of 88 variables
  • /data/acs_variable_problems.csv - Documented label inconsistencies (no data impact)

Broadband Data

Note: CORI uses FCC National Broadband Map instead of ACS broadband variables because:

  • FCC provides location-level precision (addresses)
  • ACS broadband is household survey (less precise)
  • FCC updates more frequently

Cost Burden Handling

Housing cost burden variables:

  • Calculate as % of households paying >30% of income
  • Exclude households with no/negative income (undefined burden)
  • Owner and renter burdens calculated separately

Related Datasets

  • Census Population Estimates - Annual population (no survey error)
  • QCEW Employment & Wages - Administrative employment data
  • FCC National Broadband Map - Preferred broadband source

Usage Example

library(tidycensus)

# Set API key (get from census.gov)
census_api_key("YOUR_KEY_HERE", install = TRUE)

# Get ACS data
acs_data <- get_acs(
  geography = "county",
  variables = c(
    median_income = "B19013_001",
    poverty_rate = "B17001_002"
  ),
  year = 2023,
  survey = "acs5",
  state = NULL  # All states
)
